# Over-Abstention: When a RAG Generator Refuses Answers It Has

## 1. Hook — The Product Tradeoff

Standard RAG leaderboards often collapse performance to a single aggregate metric like Fact Recall. Selecting a generator solely on this headline number suggests three leading models are interchangeable: `gpt-5-nano-2025-08-07` achieves 24.6% Fact Recall, `claude-haiku-4-5-20251001` scores 24.1%, and `gemini-2.5-flash-lite` lands at 24.0%.

This similarity is a statistical mirage. Beneath the flat Fact Recall surface lie radically divergent risk profiles, operational costs, and behavioral tendencies. One model achieves its score through cautious safety refusals, another through high-volume answering at the expense of precision, and a third occupies the middle. These models represent distinct product choices rather than equivalent options. The evaluation harness makes this multidimensional tradeoff visible, showing that model selection requires aligning generator behavior with enterprise risk tolerances.

## 2. The Three-Way Metric Landscape

To move beyond single-metric evaluations, the RAG harness collects metrics covering precision, faithfulness, abstention, and cost. While retrieval capability remains constant across baseline runs, the generators utilize context differently. The overall baseline results:

| Model                     | Fact Recall | Fact Precision | Faithfulness | Abstain Precision | Abstain Recall | Cost  |
| :------------------------ | :---------: | :------------: | :----------: | :---------------: | :------------: | :---: |
| gpt-5-nano-2025-08-07     |    24.6%    |     80.3%      |    88.1%     |       10.5%       |     69.0%      | $0.89 |
| claude-haiku-4-5-20251001 |    24.1%    |     91.4%      |    92.1%     |       9.7%        |     93.3%      | $1.70 |
| gemini-2.5-flash-lite     |    24.0%    |     78.2%      |    78.6%     |       13.6%       |     70.0%      | $0.64 |

While aggregate recall varies by only a fraction of a percent, the true divergence occurs in the quality and safety metrics. Claude Haiku stands out with a Fact Precision of 91.4% and a Faithfulness of 92.1%, but costs $1.70. Gemini Flash Lite is the most economical at $0.64, though it has lower faithfulness (78.6%). The critical dynamics of RAG evaluation reside in the specific failure modes and operational costs rather than flat recall.

## 3. The Tradeoff Axis: Abstention vs. Hallucination

The primary divergence is the tradeoff axis between abstention and hallucination. When unsure, a generator must choose: abstain or attempt an answer and risk a hallucination. Each model resolves this differently:

Claude Haiku leans heavily toward caution, registering 262 abstention errors and only 10 hallucinations. Gemini Flash Lite represents the opposite extreme, with 142 abstention errors and 46 hallucinations, showing a strong bias toward answering even when unsure. GPT-5 Nano occupies the middle with 179 abstention errors and 23 hallucinations.

To understand the mechanics, one must examine the split between Abstain Precision and Abstain Recall. Abstain Precision measures how often an abstention was correct (i.e., the question was unanswerable). Abstain Recall measures how many truly unanswerable questions were successfully caught and refused:

| Model                     | Abstain Precision | Abstain Recall |
| :------------------------ | :---------------: | :------------: |
| gpt-5-nano-2025-08-07     |       10.5%       |     69.0%      |
| claude-haiku-4-5-20251001 |       9.7%        |     93.3%      |
| gemini-2.5-flash-lite     |       13.6%       |     70.0%      |

All three models exhibit an Abstain Precision of around 10%, meaning that in approximately 90% of abstention cases, the question was answerable from the retrieved documents. However, Abstain Recall diverges sharply. Claude Haiku achieves 93.3% Abstain Recall, successfully refusing almost all truly unanswerable questions. The cost of this safety net is severe over-abstention: the model repeatedly refuses to answer questions that are fully answerable, treating valid context as insufficient. The models are tuned to different safety points, where high precision comes at the cost of high refusal rates.

## 4. Root-Cause Attribution: Proving Generator Responsibility

When an evaluation harness detects high abstention, a critical diagnostic question arises: did the retriever fail to find the relevant information, or did the generator fail to extract it? Many RAG evaluations suffer from attribution confusion, unable to isolate whether a zero-recall score is caused by a poor retrieval threshold or generator timidity. The RAG harness avoids this by tracking document overlap and layer-specific state.

This capability is crucial for analyzing the 262 abstention errors generated by Claude Haiku. A naive analysis might blame the 0.45 abstention threshold for gating out critical documents. However, the harness provides proof that the over-abstention is genuine generator behavior, not a retrieval failure.

An exhaustive query of the evaluation records reveals that 90.46% (237 out of 262) of Claude Haiku's abstention errors occurred under a specific set of conditions: retrieval did not gate the query (`did_abstain_retrieval == False`), the gold annotated document was successfully retrieved and present in the `retrieval_ranked_ids` list, and yet the generator still elected to return an empty or refusing answer (`did_abstain_e2e == True`). When applying a looser proxy that simply checks whether the retrieval output was non-empty regardless of gold overlap, the proportion rises to 99.2%.

This clear attribution relies directly on three architectural decisions detailed in the project's design documentation:

- [ADR-0008](../adr/0008-failure-taxonomy.md) (Rule-Based Failure-Mode Taxonomy and Classifier) defines the classification cascade. It prioritizes the categorization of `abstention_error` to ensure that false abstentions are isolated first before being masked by downstream recall metrics.
- [ADR-0001](../adr/0001-eval-framework.md) (Eval Framework — Custom Thin Per-Fact Judge) outlines the custom per-fact evaluation logic. This judge evaluates generated answers against atomic, gold-standard facts, verifying that the gold context indeed contained the information necessary to answer the question, thereby distinguishing an error from a justified refusal.
- [ADR-0003](../adr/0003-generation.md) (Generation Layer — OpenAI Structured Outputs with Source Attribution) establishes the generation sentinel and structured JSON output. This design makes the end-to-end abstention signal `did_abstain_e2e` explicitly observable rather than leaving it to heuristic post-processing text parsing.

Through this instrumented pipeline, the harness isolates generator behavior from retrieval performance, showing exhaustively that Claude Haiku's cautious tuning is the sole driver of its high refusal rate.

## 5. A Concrete Walkthrough: Question qst_0126

To ground this aggregate behavior, the evaluation harness allows developers to inspect individual records. The case of question `qst_0126` demonstrates the over-abstention pattern in detail.

The query submitted to the system was:
"For IronCrest Bioinformatics evaluating Redwood, what was the typical time to get from signup to receiving the first response token using the self-serve quickstart flow?"

The gold fact required for a correct answer is "about 15 to 30 minutes", documented in the source file `dsid_912991a0a2ce4000a55b1f67bda8c3a3` (categorized under basic). During baseline execution, the retriever achieved a gold overlap score of 1/1 for all three models. The exact same gold document was loaded into the prompt context for all three generators; the inputs were identical.

The three models processed this context with different results:

- GPT-5 Nano extracted the correct fact, stating that the time was 15 to 30 minutes (`failure_mode=correct`, fact_recall 1.0).
- Gemini Flash Lite also answered correctly, providing the 15-to-30-minute window.
- Claude Haiku alone abstained, outputting: _"I don't have enough information to answer this question."_ (resulting in `failure_mode=abstention_error`, `did_abstain_retrieval=False`, and `did_abstain_e2e=True`).

Two out of three models extracted the answer from the exact same retrieved context, while Claude Haiku refused. This example is not a cherry-picked anomaly; it was drawn from the 262-record pool that the 90.46% pattern quantifies.

`rag-inspect` is a read-only inspection command shipped in Phase 11. The execution of this specific example can be reproduced by running:

```bash
rag-inspect --question-id qst_0126
```

By inspecting this output, a reviewer can confirm that the retrieval layer performed flawlessly, while the generator unilaterally elected to withhold the answer.

## 6. Enterprise Implications

For organizations deploying RAG systems, these findings have significant product implications. A common pattern is to select a generator based on safety. Claude Haiku's low rate of hallucinations (only 10) makes it attractive. However, safety becomes its own failure mode when it manifests as over-abstention.

A customer support bot that responds with "I don't have enough information" to a query explicitly documented in the knowledge base erodes user trust as effectively as a wrong answer. It represents a drop in system utility, forcing users to abandon the automated system.

Selecting a generator is a nuanced product decision. Organizations must weigh the cost of a hallucination against the cost of a false refusal, while factoring in execution costs—ranging from Gemini Flash Lite's $0.64 to Claude Haiku's $1.70. A business cannot make these choices deliberately without an evaluation harness that attributes failures to the right layer. The primary differentiator of this codebase is not a state-of-the-art RAG architecture, but this robust evaluation and observability layer that transforms flat performance metrics into actionable, layer-specific diagnostics.
